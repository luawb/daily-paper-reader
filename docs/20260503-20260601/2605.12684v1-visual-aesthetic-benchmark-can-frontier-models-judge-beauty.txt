Abstract 

Multimodal large language models (MLLMs) are now routinely deployed for visual understanding, generation, and curation. A substantial fraction of these applications require an explicit aesthetic judgment. Most existing solutions reduce this judgment to predicting a scalar score for a single image. We first ask whether such scores faithfully capture comparative preference: in a controlled study with eight expert annotators, score-derived rankings align poorly with the same annotators’ direct comparisons, while direct ranking yields substantially higher inter-annotator agreement on best- and worst-image labels. Motivated by this finding, we introduce the Visual Aesthetic Benchmark (VAB), which casts aesthetic evaluation as comparative selection over candidate sets with matched subject matter. VAB contains 400 tasks and 1,195 images across fine art, photography, and illustration, with labels derived from the consensus of 10 independent expert judges per task. Evaluating 20 frontier MLLMs and six dedicated visual-quality reward models, we find that the strongest system identifies both the best and the worst image correctly across three random permutations of the candidate order in only 26.5% of tasks, far below the 68.9% achieved by human experts. Fine-tuning a 35B-parameter model on 2,000 expert examples brings its accuracy close to that of a 397B-parameter open-weight model, suggesting that the comparative signal in VAB is transferable. Together, these results expose a clear and measurable gap between current multimodal models and expert aesthetic judgment, and VAB provides the first set-based, expert-grounded testbed on which that gap can be tracked and closed.

## 1 Introduction 

Multimodal large language models (MLLMs) have become critical to a widening range of visual tasks such as image generation and curation (Liu et al., 2023; Team et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024). Many of these applications require aesthetic judgment, asking models to compare visual works and identify differences in execution quality across composition, color harmony, technical execution, and cultural convention. This makes aesthetic judgment a demanding test of higher-order multimodal reasoning that is often omitted by existing visual benchmarks (Yu et al., 2023; Yue et al., 2024). Existing image-aesthetics datasets and methods typically reduce aesthetic judgment to a mapping from an image to a scalar score (Murray et al., 2012; Kong et al., 2016; He et al., 2022; Lee and Kim, 2019; Hosu et al., 2019; Yi et al., 2023; He et al., 2023), an approach that we argue is poorly suited to the underlying decision being studied. Preference order is inferred from independent scores rather than measured through direct comparison; crowd-averaged labels mix expertise, taste, and annotation noise, so the aesthetic signal is difficult to isolate; and because images in such datasets often depict different subjects, a model can shortcut its way to high accuracy by exploiting content preferences instead of discriminating execution quality. We first ask whether scoring faithfully captures comparative preference . Comparative ranking yields 42 percentage points higher inter-annotator agreement on best-image selection than score-derived rankings (Talebi and Milanfar, 2018; Ma et al., 2017; Wu et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2024a). The two protocols therefore elicit different preference signals from the same annotators, and direct comparison captures the underlying judgment more faithfully than the pointwise scoring used in most existing pipelines. Building on this finding, we introduce the Visual Aesthetic Benchmark (VAB) , which formulates aesthetic judgment as a comparative selection problem over candidate sets with matched subject matter. VAB contains 400 tasks and 1,195 images across fine art, photography, and illustration, covering 24 topics (Figure 1). Each task presents two to six images sharing a common subject, and the model selects the best image and, under the stricter setting, also the worst image. Three design choices distinguish VAB from prior aesthetic benchmarks. Matched subject matter within each task isolates differences in execution from differences in content; each ground-truth label is the consensus of 10 independent expert judges, filtered through a dual-threshold criterion to remove ambiguous cases; and every task is evaluated under three independent random permutations of the candidate order. pass^3 , which credits a task only when the model is correct in all three. We benchmark 20 frontier MLLMs and six dedicated visual-quality reward models on VAB. The strongest system, Claude Sonnet 4.6, reaches 26.5% on TB-1 pass^3 , 42.4 points below the human baseline of 68.9%. Models are also highly sensitive to candidate order, retaining only 34–65% of their ap@1 accuracy when required to be correct across all three permutations, and they degrade much faster than humans as the candidate set grows: a 7.1 × drop from two-image to four-or-more-image tasks, against only 2.0 × for human experts. ## 2 Human Annotation Study: Ranking vs. Scoring 

Existing aesthetic benchmarks rely on Scoring to collect ground truth labels (Murray et al., 2012; Kong et al., 2016; He et al., 2022; Cao et al., 2025). We conduct a controlled human study to explore the following research question regarding the annotation process: 

Which protocol yields a more scientifically sound signal for data annotation? 

• Scoring: expert annotates each image independently on an absolute aesthetic scale. • Ranking: expert compares images and annotates their relative order. 

Setup and Metrics. Eight expert annotators evaluate the same images under two protocols: (1) scoring 

assigns each image an independent score si,a ∈ [0, 10 ]; (2) ranking orders images directly within groups of size n ∈ { 2, 3, 4, 5 }. We construct 119 homogeneous-content tasks, in which all candidates share the same underlying content, and 107 heterogeneous-content tasks formed by regrouping images across source groups while preserving topic consistency. Higher accuracy means the protocol produces ground truth labels that different experts can independently reproduce. • Distinguishability (top-1 entropy (Shannon, 1948)): we measure H = − ∑i pi log 2 pi, where pi is the fraction of annotators selecting image i as best. Scoring does not faithfully preserve preference order. Table 1 shows that score-derived rankings often diverge from the direct comparisons given by the same annotator. The mean Kendall’s τ is 0.188 for homogeneous-content and 0.351 for heterogeneous-content tasks; the top-1 self-consistency rates are 45.5% and 52.0%, respectively. In other words, nearly half of the numeric score annotations fail to recover the order that the same annotator expresses under direct comparison. 

Ranking yields more reproducible labels. Table 2 shows that ranking yields substantially higher inter-annotator agreement on best and worst labels. Ranking makes the top choice stand out more clearly. Table 3 measures how concentrated their top-choice preference is within each task. It shows that ranking produces lower entropy over top-1 selections (Shannon, 1948) (0.729 vs. 1.213) and a much higher rate of unanimous choices (10.1% vs. 0.8%; Wilcoxon

Interpretation. Our findings align with conclusions from two complementary disciplines. Aesthetically ,modern aesthetics favors a relational view of beauty (Goldman, 1995; Levinson, 2005): people judge prefer-ence more reliably when alternatives are juxtaposed than when items are assessed in isolation. Cognitively ,comparative judgment is a more primitive operation than absolute evaluation (Thurstone, 1927). Absolute scoring requires maintaining an internal reference scale and converting qualitative impressions into numbers, imposing heavier cognitive load and greater instability (Stewart et al., 2005; Parducci, 1965). We therefore adopt ranking as the annotation protocol for VAB. Extended discussion is provided in Ap-pendix F.2.

3.2 Data Construction 

Because VAB is annotated through direct ranking over candidate sets, data construction is organized around matched image groups rather than standalone images: in every domain, the members of a set share subject matter but vary in execution quality. Figure 3 summarizes the pipeline; Appendix B.2 reports the full raw-pool statistics, and Appendix B.3 contains the domain-level construction details. 

Fine Art. We commissioned 426 painting sets comprising 1,142 images. Each set is built from constrained artist prompts that preserve subject matter while allowing variation in composition, value structure, color handling, and finish. All works were commissioned specifically for VAB. 

Photography. We constructed 670 photography sets comprising 1,809 images. Each set originates from a single source photograph, many provided directly by professional photographers, and is expanded through two pipelines: an expert-edit pipeline based on professional retouching tools, which can produce either original/improved pairs or larger multi-expert sets, and a scalable agent-edit pipeline that generates improved and degraded variants. The agent-edit prompt sequence and runtime pipeline are recorded in Appendix H.1.

3.3 Expert Annotation and Ground Truth 

Following Section 2, VAB uses ranking-based expert consensus for ground truth. 

Review setup. Creation and evaluation are strictly separated. In the raw pre-filter pool, each collected candidate set is reviewed by 10 independent expert judges drawn from a pool of more than 100 evaluators, yielding over 13,000 assessments and more than 300 hours of review. Judges remain blind to artist identity throughout annotation. Rubrics and interface examples are in Appendix C. Additional topic-level examples are in Appendix I. 

Annotation protocol. Each task presents k candidate images side by side. Judges first assess each image against the domain-specific rubric and then make a comparative selection over the full set. For k = 2, a judge selects the best image. For k ≥ 3, a judge selects both the best and the worst image, with distinct choices required. Every task receives exactly 10 judgments. 4.1 Setup Evaluation scope. We evaluate 20 MLLMs and six reward models on VAB. Unless otherwise noted, each model is evaluated under its default inference configuration. We compare all systems against two baselines. The Human Expert baseline is obtained from a fresh round of annotation in which 10 expert judges evaluate each released task under the benchmark interface, and we report their average accuracy against the released labels. 4.2.1 Results for Multimodal Large Language Models 

Gap to experts. Current frontier models remain far below expert performance. On the strictest metric, TB-1 pass^3 , Claude Sonnet 4.6 attains 26.5%, against 68.9% for human experts; even under the more lenient 

TB-1 ap@1 , the strongest model, Doubao Seed 2.0 Pro, reaches 42.8%, still 26.1 points short of the expert baseline. 

Progress across families. Improvements are uneven across model families. Claude Sonnet 4.6 nearly doubles its predecessor on TB-1 pass^3 (14.5% → 26.5%), whereas the GPT-5 series regresses from 21.8% (GPT-5) to 20.0% (GPT-5.1) and 15.5% (GPT-5.2). 4.2.2 Results for Reward Models

Comparison with MLLMs. The best reward-model Top-1 result , 52.2%, is numerically close to the best MLLM Top-1 ap@1 result , 55.4%, but the comparison is not strictly like-for-like: MLLMs make a single comparative decision over a candidate set, whereas reward models score images independently and are then ranked within the set. 

4.3 Ablations and Additional Analysis Temperature ablation. For models with an exposed temperature parameter, greedy decoding improves 

Top-1 pass^3 by 1.1 points on average and TB-1 pass^3 by 2.3 points. Decoding stochasticity therefore contributes noise, but it does not explain the main performance gap; full model-by-model results are reported in Appendix E.5. 

Candidate set size. Performance degrades sharply as the candidate set grows. Under TB-1 pass^3 , the best model falls from 47.3% on two-image tasks to 6.7% on four-image tasks, while human experts decline from 87.1% to 43.6%. Larger candidate sets therefore amplify both comparative difficulty and instability; the full breakdown is reported in Appendix E.6.

## 6 Conclusion 

We introduced VAB, a benchmark that recasts aesthetic evaluation as comparative selection over candidate sets with matched subject matter, with labels grounded in expert ranking rather than scalar scoring. VAB contains 400 tasks and 1,195 images spanning fine art, photography, and illustration. A controlled human study motivates the protocol: direct ranking yields 42 percentage points higher inter-annotator agreement than score-derived rankings. Across 20 frontier MLLMs and six reward models, the strongest system reaches 26.5% on TB-1 pass^3 against 68.9% for human experts, and accuracy drops sharply as the candidate set grows; models also exhibit pronounced positional instability across permutations of the candidate order. Fine-tuning a 35B-parameter model on 2,000 expert examples brings its accuracy close to that of a 397B-parameter open-weight model, suggesting that expert comparative supervision transfers efficiently with lightweight adaptation. We see several directions for future work: broadening domain and annotator diversity beyond the present three domains, improving robustness to candidate-order permutations, and scaling expert comparative supervision to larger training corpora. By making expert aesthetic judgment a measurable, comparative target, VAB establishes the foundation on which expert-aligned multimodal aesthetic judgment can now be systematically pursued.

## A Related Work 

Multimodal Large Language Models. Recent MLLMs such as Flamingo, BLIP-2, InstructBLIP, LLaVA, Gemini, and Qwen2-VL have rapidly improved multimodal perception, instruction following, and reasoning (Alayrac et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023; Dai et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Team et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024). Their capabilities are typically assessed on broad benchmarks such as MM-Vet and MMMU (Yu et al., 2023; Yue et al., 2024), where each example usually admits a single correct answer. Aesthetic judgment is different: it is comparative, convention-sensitive, and not reducible to factual correctness alone. Our focus is therefore whether frontier MLLMs can make stable, expert-aligned selections over candidate sets with matched subject matter rather than pointwise judgments on isolated images. 

Image Aesthetics Assessment. Image aesthetics assessment (IAA) has traditionally been posed as single-image score prediction. Canonical datasets such as AVA and AADB provide large-scale photographic images with scalar ratings. Later resources extend this paradigm through comments, theme-aware labels, and personalization, including AVA-Reviews, TAD66K, and PARA (Murray et al., 2012; Kong et al., 2016; Wang et al., 2019; He et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2022). These resources support a broad line of regression and ranking models, including NIMA, A-Lamp, theme-aware predictors, and more recent vision-language scorers such as Q-Align and UniQA (Talebi and Milanfar, 2018; Ma et al., 2017; He et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2024a). Still, the dominant setup remains pointwise, mapping from one image to a score, with preference order inferred only indirectly from independent estimates. More recently, MLLM-based efforts have moved toward richer aesthetic reasoning. AesBench evaluates aesthetic perception in frontier MLLMs; AesExpert and UNIAA build instruction-tuned systems for critique and assessment; and ArtiMuse and the photography-focused The Photographer’s Eye move closer to expert-style analysis (Huang et al., 2024b;a; Zhou et al., 2024b; Cao et al., 2025; Qi et al., 2025). Nevertheless, existing resources remain largely single-image, score-centric, or domain-specific. In contrast, VAB is explicitly set-based: each example is a group with matched subject matter, and the task is to identify the strongest and weakest images under controlled content. This formulation targets relative aesthetic discrimination directly, derives labels from expert consensus rather than crowd averages, and spans fine art, photography, and illustration within a unified benchmark.

B.3 Data Construction Details Fine Art. We commissioned 426 painting sets comprising 1,142 images across nine fine-art topics: calligra-phy, Chinese painting, ink and wash, landscape color, portrait color, portrait sketch, quick sketch, still-life color, and still-life sketch. For each topic, artists received constrained prompts that fixed the subject and high-level compositional requirements while allowing variation in execution. The resulting sets preserve semantic intent while differing in composition, value structure, color harmony, and paint handling. All works were commissioned specifically for VAB rather than drawn from existing collections, which reduces the risk of contamination from publicly indexed art. Before annotation, we remove near-duplicates and discard sets with semantic mismatches or trivial cues. 

Photography. We constructed 670 photography sets comprising 1,809 images across nine topics: archi-tecture, food and product, landscape, macro, night and astrophotography, portrait, sports, street and city, and wildlife. Each set originates from a single source photograph, many provided directly by professional photographers. We transform these source images into controlled comparison sets through two pipelines. In the expert-edit pipeline , photographers and retouchers improve each source image using tools such as Adobe Photoshop and Lightroom (Adobe, 2026b;a), applying recomposition, color and tone correction, and content-aware expansion. The original photograph and its improved variant form a pair. For a subset of source images, multiple experts independently edit the same photograph, yielding larger candidate sets with matched subject matter and varying aesthetic quality. In the agent-edit pipeline , each source image is first sent to a critic model, ArtiMuse (Cao et al., 2025), which produces a detailed aesthetic review. The review and the original image are then passed to a general-purpose MLLM, GPT-5 (OpenAI, 2025b), to generate two editing prompts: one for improvement and one for degradation. These prompts, together with the original image, are fed to an image-to-image model, Gemini-3-Pro-Image (Nano Banana Pro) (Google DeepMind, 2025a), to generate an improved and a degraded variant. The original, improved, and degraded images form a three-image candidate set. The exact prompt sequence is recorded in Appendix H.1.

Illustration. We constructed 271 illustration sets comprising 908 images across six topics: anime and manga, comic, concept art, digital and AI art, pixel art, and stylized 3D. The resulting renders form candidate sets with matched subject matter and controlled variation in composition and visual presentation. All illustration sets from both pipelines are deduplicated and reviewed for semantic consistency before annotation.

## C Annotation Details 

C.1 Annotation Rubrics 

The rubric serves as a structured comparison guide rather than a separate numerical scoring form. Judges assess each image against domain-specific criteria before making their final comparative selection. The rubrics below describe the criteria used for each domain. 

Fine Art Rubric 

Given a set of 2 to 6 fine art paintings with the same style, subject matter, and medium, and only subtle differences, determine which work demonstrates greater artistic completeness and more mature aesthetic expression. 

Core Comparison Criteria 1. Composition and Visual Order 

• Is the primary subject more clearly defined? • Does the composition feel more stable or intentionally dynamic? • Is the spatial arrangement natural, without unnecessary congestion or emptiness? 

2. Color and Tonal Relationships 

• Are the color relationships more harmonious or layered? • Is there stronger control over warmth/coolness and light/dark contrast? • Does the work avoid muddiness, dullness, or scattered color impressions? 

3. Technical Control 

• Are the brushstrokes more confident, fluid, and rhythmic? • Are there signs of hesitation, redundancy, or ineffective marks? • Does the technique serve the image rather than draw attention to itself? 

4. Degree of Completion 

• Are there any areas that appear unfinished or perfunctory? • Are edges and transitions handled naturally? • Does the work feel resolved, at a point where the artist could reasonably stop? 

5. Artistic Expression and Overall Character 

• Is the mood or atmosphere more focused and coherent? • Does the work convey a stronger overall character or presence? • Does it have greater artistic persuasiveness? 

6. Final Selection 

• Considering the set as a whole, which image is the strongest work? • If the set contains three or more images, which image is the weakest work? 

Should Not Be Considered 

• Personal preference for subject matter. • Any contextual or background information beyond what is visible in the image itself.

Photography Rubric 

Given a set of 2 to 6 photographs taken of similar subjects, in similar scenes, and under closely matched conditions, determine which image is more successful in terms of photographic language and visual expression. 

Core Comparison Criteria 1. Composition and Image Structure 

• Is the subject more clearly emphasized? • Is the frame more balanced or intentionally dynamic? • Is the background cleaner and more supportive of the subject? 

2. Quality of Light 

• Is the light more natural or more expressive? • Are highlights and shadows better controlled? • Does the image avoid crushed blacks or blown highlights? 

3. Sharpness and Focus 

• Is focus more accurate where it matters? • Is the relationship between sharpness and blur more appropriate? • Are there any technical errors that detract from the image? 

4. Color or Tonal Rendering 

• Is white balance or color temperature more accurate or more intentional? • Are colors or tonal values more consistent and unified? • Does the image avoid unwanted color casts or muddy tones? 

5. Moment and Emotional Expression 

• Does the image capture a stronger or more meaningful moment? • Is the emotional or narrative content more compelling? • Does it carry a stronger sense of photographic presence? 

6. Final Selection 

• Considering the set as a whole, which photograph is the strongest image? • If the set contains three or more images, which photograph is the weakest image? 

Should Not Be Considered 

• The personal appeal or attractiveness of the subject. • Assumptions about shooting difficulty. • Assumptions about post-processing effort.

Core Comparison Criteria 1. Visual Focus and Information Clarity 

• Is the visual focal point clearer? • Does the visual flow guide the viewer more effectively? • Is there less visual noise or competition for attention?

F.1 Detailed Setup and Metric Definitions 

This subsection provides the full metric definitions summarized in Section 2. Eight expert annotators evaluate identical images under both protocols. Under scoring, each annotator independently assigns an absolute score si,a ∈ [0, 10 ] to each image. Under ranking, annotators provide a complete within-task ordering when presented with groups of size n ∈ { 2, 3, 4, 5 }. We operationalize “more scientifically sound” along three dimensions: fidelity to human comparative preference, reproducibility across annotators, and sharpness of the resulting label signal. The study comprises 119 homogeneous-content tasks drawn directly from the curated benchmark, where all candidates share the same underlying content, and 107 heterogeneous-content tasks formed by regrouping 370 images from distinct homogeneous task groups while preserving topic consistency. Each image appears in at most one regrouped task. Examples of both settings are given in Appendix F.3.

F.2 Extended Interpretation Philosophical lens. Our results echo the long-standing debate between Aesthetic Absolutism , which posits a context-free, intrinsic standard of beauty, and Aesthetic Relativism , which holds that judgments arise from comparison and context. Modern philosophical aesthetics has largely moved toward the relational view: people can reliably tell which image they prefer when alternatives are juxtaposed. 

Statistical lens. Scoring introduces an additional measurement layer. Annotators must first form an internal judgment and then translate it onto a numeric scale, after which pairwise preference is inferred from score differences. This pipeline adds calibration variance, scale-use heterogeneity, and extra measurement noise. Ranking measures the relative decision of interest more directly, which helps explain its higher consistency and sharper signal. 

Cognitive lens. Comparative judgment is a natural human operation. People can often say which of two images feels better before they can specify by how much. Absolute scoring is cognitively heavier because it requires maintaining an internal reference scale, calibrating intervals, and converting a qualitative impression into a number. That added abstraction likely makes scoring less stable than direct comparison.

F.6 Statistical Tests 

We report two-sided paired tests over tasks to assess whether the ranking and scoring protocols differ systematically. Unless otherwise noted, p-values are uncorrected for multiple comparisons. 

McNemar’s test for majority-agreement comparisons. For each task and each metric acc best , acc worst , and acc best&worst , we construct paired binary outcomes indicating whether the protocol achieves a five-of-eight majority hit. We then apply an exact two-sided McNemar test (McNemar, 1947; Fagerland et al., 2013) to the discordant pairs. • Homogeneous-content ( N = 119 tasks): p = 1.0 × 10 −11 for acc best , p = 2.4 × 10 −9 for acc worst , and

## H Prompt Templates and Scoring Interfaces 

This appendix records the prompt templates and scoring interfaces used in data construction and evaluation. We group them by usage: prompts for the photography pipeline, prompts for the illustration pipeline, prompts for MLLM evaluation, and scoring interfaces for reward-model evaluation. 

H.1 Photography Pipeline Prompts 

This subsection records the prompts used in the photography agent-edit pipeline described in Section 3. The pipeline consists of three stages: aspect-wise critique generation with ArtiMuse, edit planning with GPT through LiteLLM, and image editing with Gemini-3-Pro-Image (Nano Banana Pro). In Step 1, ArtiMuse is queried eight times independently with the same image, once per aesthetic aspect. The resulting responses are then concatenated into a single review string that is passed to the planning stage. 

H.1.1 Step 1: ArtiMuse Aspect-Wise Critique 

ArtiMuse Aspect-Wise Critique Calls 

The same image is attached in every call. The following eight prompts are sent independently: 1. Please evaluate the aesthetic quality of this image from the aspect of Composition & Design. 2. Please evaluate the aesthetic quality of this image from the aspect of Visual Elements & Structure. 3. Please evaluate the aesthetic quality of this image from the aspect of Technical Execution. 4. Please evaluate the aesthetic quality of this image from the aspect of Originality & Creativity. 5. Please evaluate the aesthetic quality of this image from the aspect of Theme & Communication. 6. Please evaluate the aesthetic quality of this image from the aspect of Emotion & Viewer Response. 7. Please evaluate the aesthetic quality of this image from the aspect of Overall Gestalt. 8. Please evaluate the aesthetic quality of this image from the aspect of Comprehensive Evaluation. 

Critique Aggregation Format 

The eight responses are concatenated into a single review string in the following format: 

[Composition & Design] <response> [Visual Elements & Structure] <response> [Technical Execution] <response> [Originality & Creativity] <response> [Theme & Communication] <response> [Emotion & Viewer Response] <response> [Overall Gestalt] <response> [Comprehensive Evaluation] <response>

H.4 Reward-Model Scoring Interfaces 

This subsection records the model-specific scoring interfaces used to evaluate reward models on VAB. Unlike MLLMs, reward models do not share a single comparative prompt. Instead, each model scores images independently following its released evaluation method, and we convert the resulting per-image scores into within-task rankings. Across all reward models, each image is scored num_trials times, the scores are averaged, and the averaged scores are used to determine the predicted best and worst images. We use the following terminology: explicit prompt denotes prompt text exposed at the call site, implicit prompt 

denotes prompt text embedded inside the model library, and no prompt denotes a pure vision scorer with no language instruction. 

H.4.1 ArtiMuse 

ArtiMuse uses an implicit prompt : the instruction is hardcoded inside the released library rather than passed from our evaluation script. 

ArtiMuse Internal Scoring Prompt 

Rate the aesthetics score of the image in 0-100. In the output format, numbers are replaced by 2 corresponding letters, and the mapping relationship is: score 0 to 25: 0-aa, 1-ab, 2-ac, 3-ad, . . . , 25-az, score 26 to 50: 26-ca, 27-cb, 28-cc, 29-cd, . . . , 50-cy, score 51 to 75: 51-da, 52-db, 53-dc, 54-dd, . . . , 75-dy, score 76 to 100: 76-ea, 77-eb, 78-ec, 79-ed, . . . , 100-ey.

> The answer only outputs 2 corresponding letters.

At runtime, the library automatically prepends an image token before the instruction. The model outputs a two-letter code, which is then decoded back to a numerical score in [0, 100 ].

H.4.2 Q-Align 

Q-Align also uses an implicit prompt . In our experiments, we use the aesthetic scorer by default; the library additionally exposes a quality scorer. 

Q-Align Internal Prompts                               

> Aesthetic scorer (used by default):
> USER: How would you rate the aesthetics of this image? <|image|> ASSISTANT: The aesthetics of the image is
> Quality scorer (alternative interface):
> USER: How would you rate the quality of this image? <|image|> ASSISTANT: The quality of the image is

The scorer reads the last-token logits over the five words excellent , good , fair , poor , and bad , applies a softmax, and computes a weighted sum with weights [1, 0.75, 0.5, 0.25, 0 ].

H.4.3 Q-Instruct 

Q-Instruct uses an explicit prompt passed through the command-line interface. 

Q-Instruct Evaluation Prompt 

> Rate the quality of the image.

The instruction is wrapped in the mplug_owl2 conversation format with a prepended DEFAULT_IMAGE_TOKEN .The default score is the logit difference between good and poor .

H.4.4 RealQA 

RealQA uses an explicit prompt . Under the AVA adapter used in our experiments, the default instruction is: 

RealQA Evaluation Prompt 

> Rate the aesthetics and quality of this image briefly.

The model generates free text, from which a numerical score in [0.0, 10.0 ] is parsed. If parsing fails, the library issues a second constrained prompt: 

RealQA Retry Prompt 

> Output only one numeric score. Range: 0.0 to 10.0. No words, no explanation.

H.4.5 PEAS 

PEAS-aes and PEAS-ava use no prompt . They are pure vision models with no language instruction. The scorer outputs a 10-bin distribution over scores from 1 to 10, and the final score is the expected value of that distribution.
